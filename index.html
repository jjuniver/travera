2. Thesis & why now

The clinical problem we're built around

Modern oncology's care-delivery model emerged in the 1950s under microscopy-era assumptions and has never been re-architected around individual tumor biology. NGS sharpened resolution but didn't escape the paradigm - it just produced smaller, better-defined population subgroups, not patient-level treatment answers.

<25%of those patients benefit
<12.5%of ICI patients benefit, despite broad use
6.3%of US adult cancer patients in treatment trials

Why now
Labor cost, speed, and domain expertise were barriers to creating this infrastructure before. Compute, data breadth, and costs are improving exponentially, and the first wave of personalized medicine is starting to emerge.

Compute & data integration

Multi-omic integration is no longer the bottleneck. Turning raw functional + molecular + immune + ctDNA into next best decisioning is a tech problem, not a research problem.

Biologically distinguishable data

High-content patient-level data - Travera's biophysical functional signal, single-cell molecular, ultra-sensitive MRD - is feasible at clinical scale today, not 5 years out.

Cost curve bending

Sequencing, manufacturing, and bespoke-therapy tooling are on a 5-year glide path that makes fully-resourced personalized care plausible at 10–100× lower cost. The window to build the infrastructure is now.

Why a startup, not an incumbent

Structural barriers in every adjacent player make this addressable by a from-scratch clinic, not an existing one:

  • AMCs over-index on guideline adherence and trial enrollment. Bigger and better the AMC, the most dependent on streamlined big pharma clinical trial pipeline vs. personalized route. Personalized treatment outside guidelines disqualifies patients from bigger trials, creating perverse incentives against tailoring.
  • Community oncology is volume- and cost-constrained, with tumor boards that exist mostly for accreditation. Specialists are rare; off-label exposure is feared.
  • Diagnostic incumbents (Tempus, Caris, Guardant, FMI, Natera) are built on volume-based reimbursement - the larger their data asset, the greater their structural disincentive to disrupt it. Economics of patient-as-customer doesn't mesh with reimbursement paradigm core to their business.
We have to Build the company that makes fully-resourced personalized care - three independent test types running in parallel, off-label combinations grounded in functional evidence, longitudinal immune-state monitoring through experimental therapy - available as a coherent, repeatable service.

3. Customer & the bimodal 600K cohort

Who the clinic is built for

The single most consequential design choice is who the customer is. We follow the established patient-as-customer framing: the patient is the customer. Every downstream decision cascades from that.

"Every patient becomes a rare-cancer patient once they're past guidelines." That isn't a marketing line - it's a structural observation. Across 14 common cancers, 18.6% of patients receive off-label treatment and 4.4% receive off-guideline treatment. The larger market isn't off-label utilization itself - it's the decision-moment need-state, where the guideline produces no confident answer. Triangulating ACS 2026 incidence (~2.11M new US cases), NCI rare-cancer share (>25%), RCRF's 400K+ framing, and the off-label/off-guideline study, the planning band lands at 600K–1M patients/year, with 700K–900K as the working base case.

Why the bimodal framing matters for the operating plan. The two halves of the cohort have genuinely different clinical needs that the same scientific stack serves at different service intensities. Serving only one half leaves the other unaddressed.

The long-tail half (sarcoma fits here) is a multi-year journey with many decision moments - initial workup, line changes at progression, recurrence, surgical re-resection moments. The clinic stays with the patient over years. The commercial vehicle is the decision episode plus the monitoring subscription; the relationship compounds.

The short-tail half (recurrent ovarian fits here) is a single high-urgency decision moment in a compressed window. Weeks, not months. The clinic delivers workup + ranked options + drug-access execution in that window. The commercial vehicle is the decision episode itself.

Serving only the long-tail leaves the high-urgency, ambiguity-at-recurrence cohort with no place to go. Serving only the short-tail leaves multi-year patients without sustained decision support. Doing both reaches the full bimodal cohort from day one and structurally avoids the "wrong product for wrong patient" failure mode. The launch-indication choice (sarcoma + recurrent ovarian) is therefore a deliberate portfolio move spanning both halves - same molecular + functional + immune stack, different service intensities and commercial cadences.

4. Product structure

The core product is a decision episode. Monitoring is a separate subscription. A capacity-limited bespoke tier exists but is explicitly not the default. The science stack is held constant for everyone; service intensity is the variable. Specific price points are deferred - see §13 for the pricing-policy framework.

Three SKUs, one science stack

Core decision episode

The product. Indication-specific molecular + functional workup, integrated decision report, tumor-board input, drug-access plan, 30-day post-decision support window.

What's in. Indication-appropriate broad DNA/RNA (ovarian: germline + somatic + HRD; sarcoma: fusion-aware); Travera functional (RTS for tumor; NOVA where ICI-relevant); standard pathology; tumor-board pre-read; access plan.

What's out. Single-cell scRNA at scale (bespoke only); whole-genome (bespoke only); travel coordination; founder-style hand-holding; international logistics.

Monitoring subscription

The product. Longitudinal serial monitoring with cohort-specific cadence; re-testing triggers; portable dashboard; tumor-board re-convene on new signal.

What's in. Per-cohort cadence (e.g., metastatic solid on tx: blood q4–6wk + imaging q8–12wk); MRD vendor-routed (Signatera / Personalis NeXT / Guardant Reveal by cohort); ctDNA; AE tracking via patient app; re-tumor-board on rising signal.

Why a subscription. Episodic billing fragments the patient relationship; subscription aligns incentives with the patient over time.

Bespoke add-ons

The product. Additional human time, deeper omics, travel/logistics, extended monitoring - for patients whose situation justifies it and whose payment supports the marginal cost.

What's in. Extra translational-scientist time; single-cell + 100+ multiplex protein where useful; deeper logistics.

What's not in. Premium dollars buy  deeper service, but don't get to buy a "better" decision.

The "whale tension": Richer patients can subsidize the platform only if they're NOT receiving a fundamentally different clinical-science stack. But the tension exists because add-ons can buy more workup (eg., use more vendors) or quicker time to therapy. We need to make sure we aren't optimizing for the wrong product.

Pricing specifics deferred

OpenFinal cash-price for the core episode is deferred. Decision will be made closer to launch, gated on first-10-case learnings and a comparable-pricing analysis. See §13 for the policy framework that constrains it.

Reimbursement is a partial offset, not the underwriting assumption

CMS national NGS coverage is limited to specific criteria (FDA-cleared NGS in advanced/recurrent/metastatic disease, CLIA lab, ordered by the treating physician); RNA-sequencing / protein analysis is often left to MAC or payer discretion. Some MRD products now have defined Medicare coverage. In practice, reimbursement offsets part of the molecular layer - not most of a concierge decision episode. We model reimbursement as a partial offset that grows as our NGS partnerships (eg., Variantyx) matures, not as a foundation for the core price.

5. Launch indications

Potential launch wedge: recurrent ovarian + rare cancer. Peritoneal/appendiceal is a conditional third only if tissue logistics + disease-specific champions are already in place.

Example indication mapping

Recurrent ovarian → short-tail / salvage half

Why it fits. Post-platinum recurrent disease is biomarker-ambiguous and high-urgency. Broad germline + somatic testing is already guideline-linked. HRD status matters clinically. Tissue is often obtainable at recurrence or secondary cytoreduction. Patients face treatment ambiguity beyond standard markers (BRCA+/HRD− vs. HRD+/BRCA− vs. HRP carry different evidence weights for PARP inhibitor, bevacizumab, retreatment platinum, ADC, immunotherapy).

What we sell. Episode-priced decision support at the recurrence decision moment. Functional response data ordering 5–6 candidate regimens. Drug-access execution on the chosen path. Short-window deliverable.

Stealth product wedge. Secondary cytoreduction decisions (cut/no-cut) are themselves decision moments where our data could inform - a possible higher-per-decision-value mode beyond drug selection.

Sarcoma → long-tail rare-histology half

Why it fits. Heterogeneous, often molecularly complex, depends on expert pathology and fusion-aware molecular workup. Recurrent fusions are central to subtype classification + treatment logic. Multi-year relationship - sarcoma patients face many decision moments over the course of disease, each one a re-decision episode plus monitoring.

What we sell. The full long-tail value proposition - initial baseline episode, then ongoing monitoring, then re-decision episodes at progressions. Patients are with us for years; LTV scales with monitoring subscription.

Why not a single-tissue functional-only sell. Sarcoma requires expert pathology + broad DNA/RNA with strong fusion capability up front. Functional is additive where fresh tissue is feasible; not a substitute for the molecular stack.

6. Patient journey - 6 modules

The clinic operates as 6 sequential modules that loop on progression. Each module has a service, a stack, a billing surface, and operational unknowns that we resolve in the seed window.

1

Intake / coordination

What happens. Two intake paths: (a) new / first-touch - capture the patient at the earliest diagnostic biopsy. Most valuable channel; data substrate starts here. We need to be the reflex test or partnered with the diagnostic flow at the AMC. (b) Established / late-line - retrieve banked tissue + FastQ files from past sequencing vendors. Patient has legal right to request. RCRF has this muscle.

Operational requirement. AMC handoff is a pre-agreed tissue triage pathway, not a post hoc ask. Seven embedded relationships per AMC: treating oncologist, IR or surgeon, pathology director + pathologist assistant, research coordinator, biobank/specimen manager, courier/logistics lead, molecular-core or send-out interface. If we don't have named people in each node at each AMC, the tissue pipeline isn't real. Confidence: high.

2

Testing

Sub-components. Sample acquisition (often multiple time points: diagnostic biopsy, resection, repeat biopsy, blood) and testing menu (molecular: panel/WES/WGS/RNA-seq/single-cell; functional: Travera on live tissue, 24h window; blood-based: ctDNA, MRD, immune function, methylation).

Operational note. Functional and molecular can't run on the same tissue at the same time-point. Molecular comes first to inform the drug list for functional testing, which requires live tissue within ~24 hours, so the drug panel has to be pre-selected before the resection biopsy lands. The decisive distinction for AMC cores is CLIA, not IRB: PI + IRB supports research, but a non-CLIA research core can't return clinical results, so AMC cores are usable directly only when running in CLIA-certified clinical mode for that assay and return path. On the cell-free question, the robust architecture is split-biopsy / split-specimen plus matched blood plasma, not "cell-free from the same biopsy" as the primary genomics substrate. Confidence: high on sequencing-then-functional ordering and the CLIA distinction, medium on the split-specimen architecture.

3

Assessment

What happens. Multi-source data becomes a prioritized action list. Not a single point in time - integrated across diagnosis, resection, and follow-up. The deliverable is a tumor-board input, not a competing report.

Operational note. Build the clinical core in-house, buy commodity components, and keep v1 human-supervised and tumor-board-native. Stage-one flow: clinical intake → molecular evidence retrieval → hypothesis generation → functional test selection → ranked therapy list → human tumor-board review → patient-facing report and access plan. Position the deliverable as a tumor-board INPUT, not a competitor: a structured pre-read covering clinical question, sample provenance, ranked options, evidence level, access route, and monitoring plan, with local or shared MTB adjudicating. That framing lowers physician defensiveness and makes the product additive. Confidence: high on architecture and positioning, medium on team shape.

The rare-vs.-known split. Rare-cancer / post-guideline patients: oncologists actually want our data (no NCCN block to fall back on). Known-cancer early-line: monitoring value + within-guideline prioritization (e.g., FOLFOX vs. FOLFIRI); receptivity is lower unless we make it easy.

4

Access & treatment planning

What happens. Translate ranked options into actual drug access via the 4-lane architecture (see §10). The hardest module operationally: oncologists push back when our report shows 5 strong responders that aren't covered - it becomes their job to explain to the patient why they can't have it. We own that explanation and the access execution.

Operational note. Drug access is a hybrid function. Internalize the high-judgment work (sponsor outreach, medical-necessity narratives, single-patient IND logic, payer appeal strategy, patient communication) and outsource the back-office (routine benefit verification, specialty-pharmacy routing, routine prior-auth, international logistics). Team shape for the first tens of cases: one senior access lead plus 2–3 access specialists. Bespoke manufacturing is a later question, not a launch question; if engaged at launch at all, only through selected partners for protocolized pilots, never as a core service promise. The launch thesis is the decision layer, and shouldn't be buried under manufacturing complexity. Confidence: high on the hybrid structure, medium on headcount, medium-low on the manufacturing call.

5

Monitoring

What happens. Tumor burden (ctDNA / MRD, methylation / tissue-of-origin, imaging at standard cadence) + immune function (Travera ICI-related changes, AE surveillance, chemo recovery tracking). Vanderbilt histotripsy study is the live proof point - monthly blood collections paired with immune function + ctDNA.

Operational note. Proscia is not MRD (it's digital pathology). The real MRD shortlist is Signatera (Natera; personalized tumor-informed ctDNA), Personalis NeXT Personal Dx (ultra-sensitive tumor-informed), Guardant Reveal (tissue-free, when tissue-free is strategically important), and clonoSEQ (Adaptive; only FDA-cleared for MM/CLL/ALL), with Foresight and Veracyte as emerging options. There's no single "right" monitoring cadence; defaults need to be cohort-specific, tied to disease biology and treatment landmarks. Practical launch cadence: metastatic solid on active tx → blood q4–6wk + imaging q8–12wk; post-definitive high-risk → ctDNA/MRD q8–12wk for 2yrs then less; myeloma/heme → defined response landmarks then q3–6mo in maintenance. Confidence: high on the MRD shortlist excluding Proscia, medium-low on the final pick and cadence specifics.

6

Repeat / iterate

What happens. The loop. When monitoring flags progression, we already have the substrate to act on the next decision rapidly. The benchmark framing: at any progression, a fully-resourced patient has ~20 things ready to try, escalated by seriousness. Our job is to generate that ranked list automatically from the patient's substrate.

Operational note. Arsenal curation runs as hybrid editorial: machine ingestion plus expert human curation plus tumor-board consumption. A continuously updated evidence and opportunity graph fed by literature, approvals, trials, sponsor access policies, and internally observed outcomes; AI generates, clinicians adjudicate. Own the longitudinal dashboard product, but make it interoperable from day one. RCRF is an integration partner, not the product core, and ONC FHIR plus Cures Act information-blocking rules favor a standards-based portable architecture. Vendor websites don't become the live database; instead, versioned test menus, connectors, and change-control. Confidence: medium on curation operating model, high on the dashboard ownership and interoperability stance.

7. Sample & lab logistics

The architecture

Three layers, each with a defined owner:

Tissue supply line - RCRF

RCRF (Rare Cancer Research Foundation) is the tissue acquisition + bio-banking + in-house pathology + clinical-history retrieval partner. Patient-powered model; strong rare-disease cohort access. Layered legal structure: MSA + tissue/custodial agreement + DUA/BAA + optional FMV secondment. Not equity - IRS inurement rules prohibit shareholder benefit in a 501(c)(3).

Functional layer - Travera (in-house)

Travera SMR biophysical platform. CLIA-certified in 50 states + NYS-CLEP. RTS (tumor, 2-day TAT) + NOVA (immune, 3-day TAT, blood-based). >100 FDA-approved drugs in library. Non-destructive; compatible with parallel scRNA/proteomics.

Molecular layer - NGS partner (category to fill)

The slot, not the name. What we need is a reliable way to get NGS done. The partnership doesn't have to be exclusive, and the partner doesn't have to be one specific vendor. Variantyx is the closest current candidate (WGS-oncology, distributed-CLIA model, negotiation in flight), but the same slot can be filled by anyone on the RFP shortlist (Tempus xE/xR, Personalis NeXT Dx, Caris WES/WTS, FoundationOne CDx). Upside if Variantyx lands: potential colocation with Travera to share CLIA overhead. Nice-to-have, not a requirement. Distributed-CLIA feasibility requires the clinical performing + reporting lab stays CLIA-certified and accountable; timeline 6–12mo on a mature workflow, 12–18mo for new assays.

Pre-procedure tissue SOPs (not "leftover" tissue)

Solve tissue scarcity with pre-procedure SOPs; never plan on "leftover tissue." Federal CLIA retention requires pathology remnants preserved ≥2 years; CAP commonly requires paraffin blocks ~10 years - that's why AMC pathology is conservative about release. Operational fix: disease- and procedure-specific tissue SOP defining (a) diagnostic reserve, (b) live-cell aliquot priority, (c) backup FFPE reserve, (d) who signs off when tissue is scarce.

Standardized RFP template with our exact launch sample mix, volumes, TAT requirements, indemnities, PHI/data-rights terms, failure-remake policies - sent to all five sequencing shortlist candidates. 9. Tumor board model

Posture decision. Position our report as a tumor-board input, not a competitor. Produce a structured pre-read; let the local or shared MTB adjudicate. ESMO's recent molecular-tumor-board recommendations focus on structure, workflow, documentation, quality indicators, follow-up - exactly the posture we want. Lowers physician defensiveness; product becomes additive. Confidence: high.

The structured pre-read format

Every tumor board input we produce contains the same six elements, regardless of indication:

  1. Clinical question. What decision is being made and what evidence threshold should it be made against?
  2. Sample provenance. Which tissue, what date, what processing, what assays run, with which vendor/lab.
  3. Ranked options. Top candidate therapies with rationale - molecular signal, functional signal where available, mechanism logic.
  4. Evidence level. Per option: NCCN level, peer-reviewed strength, off-label vs. on-label, single-patient IND viability.
  5. Access route. Per option: insurance-covered, off-label-with-appeal, comp-use, sponsor program, trial enrollment, dormant-asset route.
  6. Monitoring plan. What we'll watch post-decision and what triggers re-tumor-board.

10. Drug access architecture

Drug access is the module where the clinic does what an oncologist's office can't - it finds and obtains the drug. This is one of the strongest sources of patient lock-in and one of the operationally hardest functions. Hybrid model: internalize judgment, outsource routine.

The 4 access lanes

Lane 1 - Direct sponsor expanded access / compassionate use

Negotiate directly with pharma's medical-affairs team. Clinic-level pre-existing relationships make this faster than ad-hoc patient-by-patient outreach. Drug-donation flywheel: clinic trades outcomes data for donated supply - pharma gets clinical learning outside randomized trials, patient gets drug, clinic keeps data.

Lane 2 - Managed-access specialists

Outsourced routes for cross-border access, complex logistics, and breadth across many sponsors at once. Shortlist: myTomorrows, Clinigen, Inceptua. Pick 1–2 preferred routes for the first 25 patients - a near-term deliverable.

Lane 3 - Repurposing / dormant-asset routes

19. Risk register

Operational risks

Multi-state telemedicine prescribing complexity. CA and NY are non-IMLC; each requires independent licensure for each employed oncologist. The clinic needs internal counsel + licensing operations function from day 1 - none of the original seed documents quantified this with sufficient depth. Mitigation: hire ops lead with prior multi-state telemed experience as priority T1 hire; outside counsel retained for state-medical-board work from close.
Team retention at Travera. Rob has flagged that another short sprint without commitment will likely break the team. The seed-round timing is itself a retention lever. Mitigation: close the seed expeditiously; deliver Rob the agency he's flagged (Option A path preferred); equity refresh structured into the close.
AMC tissue-triage relationships not real until named-person mapping is complete. "If we don't have named people in each of seven embedded roles at each AMC, the tissue pipeline isn't real yet." Mitigation: AMC named-person mapping is an explicit T1 deliverable, not a research question.
Senior clinical lead + senior access lead hiring delays. Both are T1 priority hires; the JD/MD profile for the access lead is narrow. Mitigation: KV recruiting network active from day 1; back-channel candidates identified pre-close; fractional outside-counsel option as interim access lead while permanent search runs.

Clinical / scientific risks

Functional + molecular timing conflict. Functional requires live tissue within ~24 hours; drug panel must be pre-selected before the resection biopsy lands. If the workflow doesn't synchronize, the functional layer is degraded. Mitigation: pre-procedure tissue SOPs per indication; "drug panel pre-loaded by molecular pre-read" workflow built into the platform v0 spec.
Decision-change rate isn't dramatic enough. Our recommendation diverges from local plan in <X% of cases (X to be determined empirically) - if too low, the case for any premium cash-pay price weakens. Mitigation: first-10-case learning specifically calibrates this rate; if low, the indication wedge or workup depth may need adjustment before scaling.
Tissue scarcity / "leftover tissue" trap. CLIA retention requires pathology remnants preserved ≥2 years; CAP often requires paraffin blocks ~10 years. AMC pathology is conservative about release. Mitigation: pre-procedure tissue SOPs (diagnostic reserve, live-cell aliquot priority, FFPE backup, sign-off rules) drafted in T1, codified per indication; AMC tissue audit pilot validates the SOPs empirically.

Commercial risks

Patient acquisition slower than modeled. 75–100 episodes in 18 months requires AMC + RCRF + selective concierge channels firing. Mitigation: three independent channels reduce single-channel risk; mid-T1 reserves include marketing contingency; if patient acquisition is significantly behind plan at month 9, redirect spend or revisit indication wedge.
Reimbursement offset doesn't materialize on schedule. CMS national NGS coverage limited; Variantyx distributed-CLIA reporting timeline is 6–12 months on mature workflow. Mitigation: reimbursement modeled as partial offset only, not underwriting assumption; the core cash-pay model holds regardless.
Pharma services SOWs slow to materialize. Drug-donation flywheel takes 6–12 months of relationship-building before donations flow predictably; pharma services contracting takes longer. Mitigation: senior access lead's first 12 months explicitly include relationship density; first pharma SOW is a month 12–18 milestone, not a month 6 milestone.

Structural / strategic risks

Cap-table cleanup cost discovery negative. If buyout negotiation with existing Travera investors comes in higher than expected (or fails entirely), Option A path collapses. Mitigation: cleanup is scoped separately from the $12–15M operating seed; operating plan can execute under Option B if cleanup is intractable. Begin negotiations early; lock buyout valuations before commitment to Option A.
NGS-partner risk (Variantyx as lead candidate). The NGS slot isn't filled yet. Variantyx is the closest to confirmed but not signed, and even if signed, their commercial position is "solid for now" (Quest partnership keeps cancer program alive) rather than a forever bet. Mitigation: treat the slot as non-exclusive by design; the RFP shortlist (Tempus xE/xR, Personalis, Caris, FMI) is real backup routing, not a fallback; Travera lab is in-house and CLIA-certified independently, so a Variantyx pivot or sell-off disrupts the reimbursement timeline but not the ability to operate.

Differentiation rests on Travera (functional) + RCRF (tissue) + KV portfolio (drug supply). Mitigation: move quickly; defensibility is in the data accumulation, not the idea.
